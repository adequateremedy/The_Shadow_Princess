The storm broke shortly before dawn.
Not gradually.
Not gently.
One final crash of thunder rolled across the city and then silence followed.
Heavy clouds drifted westward.
Rain softened.
The world emerged dripping and gray beneath the first pale light of morning.
Morrigan stood at the apartment window.
The city looked washed clean.
Fresh.
New.
The illusion hurt.
Because nothing had been washed away.
Not fear.
Not greed.
Not the hunters moving somewhere beyond the horizon.
The world only looked cleaner.
The truth remained exactly where it had been.
Dagda finished securing the last travel satchel.
The apartment felt strangely empty now.
Years of living reduced to what could fit upon wagons and backs.
The realization felt wrong.
How could an entire life become so small?
How could so many memories leave behind so little weight?
Morrigan looked around the room one final time.
The chair beside the fire.
The worn bookshelf.
The uneven floorboards she once complained about and later grew to love.
The ceiling that leaked during spring storms.
The narrow kitchen.
The bedroom.
Home.
A word carrying too much meaning.
Too much history.
Too much pain.
She swallowed hard.
Then turned away.
Because looking longer would only make leaving harder.
________________________________________
The bakery smelled exactly as it always had.
Fresh bread.
Warm flour.
Butter.
Comfort.
The scent greeted them the moment they descended the stairs.
For an instant Morrigan almost forgot.
Almost believed it was another ordinary morning.
Then she saw the packed supplies near the door.
Reality returned.
Eamon was already awake.
Of course he was.
The old baker stood beside the ovens pretending to organize trays.
Pretending not to watch them.
Pretending this was normal.
No one believed the performance.
Least of all him.
Dagda approached first.
"Eamon."
The baker grunted.
Busy.
Very busy.
Entirely occupied with absolutely nothing.
"Eamon."
Another grunt.
Morrigan almost smiled.
Finally the baker turned.
His eyes looked tired.
Red around the edges.
As though sleep had been scarce.
Or nonexistent.
Neither commented.
The silence stretched.
Then Eamon crossed the room and embraced Dagda.
The gesture happened so suddenly neither appeared prepared for it.
They held each other tightly.
Briefly.
Then Eamon stepped back.
Immediately he embraced Morrigan too.
The same way.
The same fierce sincerity.
No words.
None were even necessary.
When he finally released her, his eyes settled briefly upon her stomach.
His expression softened.
"Take care of this little girl."
Morrigan nodded.
Unable to trust her own voice.
Eamon cleared his throat.
Then immediately became interested in oven temperatures.
An obvious retreat.
A necessary one.
Because farewells were difficult.
Because grief often arrived disguised as practicality.
________________________________________
They left shortly after sunrise.
Not alone.
Several other Faction members traveled with them.
Others would join later along predetermined routes.
The roads had already been planned, the shelters prepared.
The escape was organized as thoroughly as possible.
Still, uncertainty lingered.
No plan survived reality unchanged.
Dagda understood that.
Morrigan did too.
The wagon wheels rolled slowly across wet cobblestones.
The familiar streets passed by.
Each one carrying memories.
The textile shop.
The fountain.
The market square.
The bookstore.
Every corner whispered something.
Every building felt like a farewell.
People watched from windows.
From doorways.
From shops.
Some waved.
Some simply observed.
Others lowered their heads respectfully.
No one tried to stop them.
No one asked questions.
The city seemed to understand.
The city seemed to mourn.
Morrigan looked back only once.
The bakery stood exactly where it always had.
Small.
Ordinary.
Beloved.
Eamon stood in the doorway.
Arms folded.
Watching.
The distance between them grew steadily.
Yet he remained there.
Until the wagon turned a corner.
Until the bakery disappeared from sight.
Until Cogsbin finally let them go.
________________________________________
The countryside beyond the city felt strangely peaceful.
Rolling fields stretched toward distant hills.
Windmills turned lazily beneath cloudy skies.
Farmhouses dotted the landscape.
Birds circled overhead.
Life continued.
As though nothing had changed.
As though the world were not unraveling.
Morrigan sat beside Dagda at the front of the wagon.
The road curved gently through farmland.
For a while neither spoke.
The rhythm of travel settled around them.
Wheels turning.
Harnesses creaking.
Hooves striking damp earth.
Simple sounds.
Familiar sounds.
Eventually Morrigan glanced sideways.
"What are you thinking about?"
Dagda considered lying.
Then abandoned the idea immediately.
"Cogsbin."
A faint smile touched her lips.
"Me too."
The answer felt comforting.
Sad.
But comforting.
Because grief shared became lighter somehow.
Not smaller.
Just easier to carry.
________________________________________
They reached the first safe house shortly after sunset.
A secluded farmhouse hidden among hills.
Owned by a human family loyal to the Shadow Folk.
The arrangement surprised no one.
Many humans still helped.
Many still remembered friendship.
Many still refused fear.
That truth mattered.
It deserved to be remembered.
Especially now.
The family welcomed them warmly.
Food appeared almost immediately.
Stew.
Bread.
Tea.
Simple things.
Necessary things.
The hospitality felt genuine.
Not obligated.
Not political.
Human kindness remained stubbornly alive.
Even as the world darkened around it.
That night Morrigan slept deeply for the first time in weeks.
No nightmares.
No anxiety.
Only exhaustion.
Only rest.
Only the brief illusion of safety.
________________________________________
The illusion shattered two days later.
The road leading west wound through dense woodland.
Tall trees crowded both sides.
Their branches intertwined overhead.
The sunlight reaching the forest floor appeared fragmented.
Broken into shifting patches of gold and shadow.
The travelers moved quietly.
Carefully.
Conversation had diminished since leaving Cogsbin.
Everyone remained alert.
Everyone understood the risks.
The first warning came from the shadows.
Not words.
Not even sounds.
A sensation.
The darkness beneath nearby trees shifted uneasily.
Listening.
Watching.
Concerned.
Dagda felt it immediately.
His posture straightened.
The reins tightened in his hands.
Morrigan noticed.
"What is it?"
He scanned the woods.
The feeling intensified.
Something was wrong.
Close.
Very close.
Then came the sound.
A snapping branch.
Sharp.
Distinct.
The forest fell silent.
Instantly.
Birdsong vanished.
Wind seemed to disappear.
Even the horses sensed it.
Their ears flattened.
Their bodies tensed.
Every traveler froze.
Waiting.
Listening.
The next sound arrived from the left.
Movement.
Fast movement.
Then another.
And another.
The woods were suddenly alive.
Not with animals.
They remained silent.
Dagda's heart sank.
Hunters.
________________________________________
The attack happened quickly.
Too quickly.
Figures emerged between trees carrying rifles and nets.
Humans.
Armed humans.
Prepared humans.
They moved with confidence.
Organization.
Purpose.
This was not random violence.
This was a planned operation.
The realization chilled Morrigan.
They had known where to look.
Known whom to seek.
One hunter shouted.
Another raised a weapon.
Panic exploded across the road.
Horses screamed.
Travelers scattered.
The forest erupted into chaos.
Dagda moved instantly.
The shadows answered.
Darkness surged across the road.
Not attacking.
Concealing.
Swallowing visibility.
Turning certainty into confusion.
The hunters cursed.
Several stumbled.
Others fired blindly.
The sound of gunfire echoed through the trees.
Morrigan's pulse thundered.
The child.
Her mind immediately found only one thought.
Protect the child.
Everything else became secondary.
Everything.
A net flew through the darkness.
Missed narrowly.
The shadows caught it.
Twisted it aside.
Another shot rang out.
Then another.
Dagda appeared beside her.
His expression was terrifyingly calm.
"We move."
Not a suggestion.
An order.
Morrigan nodded.
The shadows thickened around them.
A living veil.
Protective.
Responding.
The road disappeared behind darkness.
The forest swallowed sound.
Swallowed sight.
Swallowed pursuit.
The hunters shouted somewhere beyond the blackness.
Confused.
Disoriented.
Angry.
They were forced to retreat, deeper into the woods.
Moving quickly.
Moving silently.
Moving together.
The darkness guided them.
Protected them.
Carried them away from danger.
For now.
Only for now.
________________________________________
Night had fallen by the time they finally stopped.
The group gathered within a hidden grove far from the road.
Breathing hard.
Exhausted.
Shaken.
No one had died.
A miracle.
A genuine miracle.
Several injuries.
Several close calls.
But no deaths.
The realization brought relief.
Brief relief.
Because another truth quickly followed.
The hunters knew.
Not rumors.
Not suspicions.
Knowledge.
They were actively searching.
Actively tracking.
Actively pursuing.
The war had entered a new stage.
Dagda sat beside a small, concealed fire.
Silent.
Thoughtful.
Morrigan settled beside him.
Their child moved faintly within her.
A reassuring flutter.
Alive.
Safe.
For now.
She rested her head against his shoulder.
Neither spoke.
The forest surrounded them.
Dark.
Ancient.
Watchful.
Far away, an owl called through the night.
The sound lingered.
Then faded.
Dagda stared into the darkness beyond the firelight.
Toward Steelmills.
Toward Crudsder.
Toward whatever future waited ahead.
Cogsbin already felt impossibly distant.
Like another life.
Like another world.
The road behind them had closed.
The road ahead remained uncertain.
And somewhere, beyond SteelMills, a sanctuary awaited.
Crudsder.
The city of woods.
A city of shadows.
The city where destiny was already moving toward them.
The city where their daughter would be born.
Though none of them knew it yet.
Not even the shadows.
