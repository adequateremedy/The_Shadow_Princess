They left Steelmills under clouded skies.
No farewells accompanied their departure.
No lingering looks.
No final walks through familiar streets.
Unlike Cogsbin, Steelmills had never become home.
The city felt less like a place and more like an open wound.
Every hour spent there deepened the sting of reality.
Every discovery stripped away another illusion.
Leaving brought no grief, only urgency, only relief.
Only the quiet understanding that remaining any longer would invite disaster.
The roads westward were crowded.
Not with merchants.
Not with ordinary travelers.
With refugees.
But Fae; dozens… then hundreds.
The farther they traveled, the more appeared.
Families carrying what bundles they could.
Children wrapped in blankets.
Elder Fae supported by younger fae hands.
Entire lives reduced to what could be carried.
The wagons were all but gone, sprinkled here and there, within the crowd.
The sight repeated endlessly.
Every hill revealed another group.
Every woodland path concealed another camp.
The exodus had begun.
And still… most humans remained unaware.
Those who noticed rarely understood what they were witnessing.
But among the Fae, the truth spread rapidly.
Every town and village everywhere, was being taken over by three major Corporations –
Aurelium Industrial Conglomerate was located here, in Steelmills. 
The A.I.C. was the central authority over all operations. They owned and controlled the
other two - Gearlight Extraction Company (which had taken over Cogsbin right after they 
left) and Blackvein Supply Syndicate, which transported, smuggled, and distributed the 
refined Essence disguised as coal freight, globally. They were located in Crudsder.
Gearlight Extraction Company controlled the front line capture and draining of Fae; labeled as “mining and research” for their public cover.
Midgard had become - no longer safe… not for them.
Perhaps not for any fae.
________________________________________
Morrigan watched the refugees constantly.
Every face seemed familiar somehow.
Not because she knew them.
Because she recognized the emotion.
Fear, exhaustion, determination, and even grief.
The same emotions lived inside her.
Inside all of them.
One afternoon the caravan stopped near a stream.
The pause was brief.
Necessary.
Horses required water.
Travelers required rest.
The road ahead remained long.
Morrigan wandered toward the water's edge.
The stream flowed clear and cold over smooth stones.
Its quiet murmur contrasted sharply with the tension surrounding the camp.
For a few precious moments she simply listened.
Water, wind, birdsong. They were simple sounds; ancient sounds.
The kind that existed before Kingdoms.
Before Courts.
Before Wars.
Before… greed.
A small voice interrupted her thoughts.
"Are you one of them?"
Morrigan looked down.
A child stood nearby.
Perhaps eight years old.
A Fae girl.
Thin.
Dirty.
Wide-eyed.
The child pointed toward the shadows gathered near Morrigan's feet.
Not afraid.
Curious.
Morrigan smiled softly.
"One of who?"
"The Shadow People."
The answer carried wonder rather than suspicion.
Morrigan crouched.
Meeting the girl's eyes.
"Why do you ask?"
The child considered.
Then shrugged.
"They say you are helping us."
The simplicity nearly broke her heart.
Because it sounded so innocent.
So absolute.
As though helping people were something extraordinary… or rare.
Morrigan reached out gently.
Brushed a leaf from the child's hair.
"We try."
The girl nodded.
Satisfied.
As though that answered everything.
Perhaps it did, for the child with hope still gleaming in her eyes.
Children often required fewer complications than adults.
The girl pointed toward Morrigan's stomach.
"My mother says there's a baby in your tummy."
Morrigan laughed softly.
"Your mother talks too much."
The child giggled.
The sound felt precious.
A reminder that innocence still existed.
Even now.
Even here.
________________________________________
The forests began appearing a few days later.
At first only scattered woodlands.
Small groves interrupting the open countryside.
Then larger stands of ancient trees.
Denser, older growth.
The landscape had gradually transformed, and the change felt immediate.
The air cooled and birdsongs increased.
The roads narrowed and nature reclaimed space the industries had not yet touched.
Every traveler noticed, and soon - hope spread quietly through the group of travelers.
Crudsder - the name passed from voice to voice.
Whispered and repeated, like it was a cherished label.
The final city that still held an untouched forest, a sanctuary.
The place where many believed safety would still exist.
Morrigan wanted to believe it too, so desperately.
Yet experience made belief difficult.
Too much had happened already.
Too many promises had failed.
Still, the woodlands helped.
Even she could not deny that.
The shadows seemed calmer here.
Less restless, and more settled.
As though recognizing something familiar.
Something ancient… something waiting.
________________________________________
Dagda noticed the pull first.
Not physically.
Instinctively.
A sensation lingering at the edge of awareness.
The deeper they traveled into the woodlands surrounding the edge of Crudsder, the stronger it became.
The pressure became magnetic.
Subtle but persistent.
The shadows reacted by gathering more frequently.
Moving without command.
Leaning and listening toward the west.
Toward the heart of the forest.
Toward something unseen.
He mentioned it to Morrigan one evening beside a small campfire.
The surrounding woods glowed silver beneath moonlight.
Fireflies flickered in the trees, like glitter.
Most were already sleeping, and only a few remained awake; keeping watch.
They were listening and waiting.
Morrigan stared into the flames.
Then she nodded slowly.
"I feel it too."
The admission surprised him.
Dagda looked up.
"You do?"
She rested her hand against her stomach.
Their child had grown noticeably during the journey.
Not large.
But undeniable.
Life was increasingly visible beneath her clothing.
The sight still amazed him, still frightened him, and yet it still filled him with wonder.
Morrigan watched the fire.
Carefully considering her words.
"It feels..."
She hesitated.
Searching.
"Familiar."
The answer deepened the silence.
Because Dagda understood exactly what she meant.
Not familiar through memory.
Something older.
Something deeper.
Recognition without explanation.
The shadows drifted around the edge of the camp.
Watching the darkness beyond the trees.
Watching whatever waited ahead.
________________________________________
The first hunters appeared the next morning.
Not attacking but merely watching.
That distinction mattered.
Barely.
Then a scouting party spotted them shortly after dawn.
Armed human riders, moving cautiously through the hills.
Observing refugee routes.
Tracking movement.
Gathering information.
The report spread quickly.
Fear followed immediately.
Travel accelerated.
Rest periods were shortened.
No one argued.
No one complained.
The danger remained too obvious.
The hunters had reached this far west.
Which meant nowhere remained untouched.
The realization settled heavily across the caravan.
Hope survived even deeper into the forest.
But certainty died.
________________________________________
By sunset they reached the outer settlements of Crudsder.
The city differed dramatically from both Cogsbin and Steelmills.
The villages were smaller, they felt older… quieter.
The buildings appeared woven into the landscape rather than imposed upon it.
Wood dominated construction.
Stone foundations.
Moss-covered roofs.
Winding roads that were not paved, or bricked, but rather worn - powdered dirt - from 
tread. Everything seemed softer somehow.
More natural, and perhaps a bit less ambitious.
The great woodlands surrounded nearly every side.
An ocean of trees stretching toward the horizon.
Ancient and vast, yet alive.
The sight stole Morrigan's breath.
Not because of beauty.
Though it was beautiful, but because of scale.
The woods seemed endless.
A living wall, that had once stood protecting the city of Crudsder.
Or concealing it.
Perhaps both.
Beside her, Dagda felt the same thing.
The magnetic pull intensified immediately.
The moment the forest came fully into view.
The shadows reacted by gathering more densely.
Watching beneath the trees, branches, leaves…
They were listening and waiting.
As though hearing distant voices.
As if they were recognizing family.
________________________________________
Crudsder overflowed.
The city was already beyond capacity.
Refugees occupied every available space.
Homes, barns, warehouses, temples, and even public buildings.
Entire streets transformed into temporary Fae camps.
The situation should have felt chaotic, but instead it felt determined.
Humans were here – helping Fae – organizing, sharing, cooperating – as they always had.
Fear for their friends united them.
At least for now.
Morrigan spent much of the following day assisting families.
Distributing supplies.
Organizing sleeping areas.
Comforting frightened children.
The work distracted her, temporarily.
Yet beneath every task lingered the same question.
What happens next?
No one seemed to know.
No one possessed answers.
Only hopes.
Only plans.
Only prayers.
________________________________________
The answer arrived three nights later.
Not from the Fae Court heads – King Oberon of the Seelie Court, or Mab of the UnSeelie Court – who had barely made it out of Steelmills with their own lives.
Not from Faction leaders – who had lead their members to these very woods.
Not even from the shadows themselves.
The evening had been unusually still.
No wind, or rain.
Not even songs from the birds that filled the branches of every tree surrounding them.
The forest surrounding Crudsder seemed strangely silent.
As if it too were listening.
The sensation unsettled everyone.
Though very few understood why.
Morrigan awoke abruptly shortly after midnight.
Her pulse raced.
Her breathing uneven.
The child moved strongly within her.
Stronger than ever before.
Pain accompanied it.
Not sharp.
Not yet.
But present.
She sat upright immediately.
Dagda woke moments later.
Concern flashed across his face.
"Morrigan?"
She pressed her hand against her stomach.
The sensation came again.
A tightening - deep and powerful.
Her eyes widened.
Dagda immediately understood.
Fear and wonder collided across his expression.
"No."
The word emerged softly.
Disbelieving.
Another contraction followed.
Stronger this time.
The tent they shared - seemed to tilt.
The shadows surged instantly.
Pouring into the tent from every corner - gathering around them.
Responding and watching.
The entire tent filled with dark shadows, and felt different suddenly.
Charged and alive.
As though reality itself had become aware that the daughter of the shadows would be 
arriving soon. Morrigan gripped Dagda's hand.
Pain flared again, and her breath got caught in the intensity.
Their daughter moved around, pushing at Morrigan’s skin, with violent twists and kicks.
The shadows darkened.
Outside, somewhere beyond Crudsder, the ancient forest awakened.
Branches creaked.
Leaves rustled.
Roots shifted beneath the earth itself, and a ripple spread through the woods.
Far beyond sight.
Far beyond understanding.
As though the world itself had felt the child stirring.
As though the realms were waiting for what they already knew was coming.
And deep within the darkness of the great forest, something impossible began preparing a path.
A path no Fae had ever walked.
A path between realms.
A path born from Akasha.
A path born from shadow.
A path born from love.
And labor had only just begun.
