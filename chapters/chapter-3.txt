Spring arrived upon Cogsbin with rain.
Not storms.
Not violent downpours.
Gentle rains that lingered for days and left the city shining beneath silver skies.
The rooftops glistened.
The cobblestones reflected lanternlight after dusk.
Window boxes overflowed with blossoms whose colors seemed brighter after every shower.
Morrigan loved the rain.
She always had.
There was honesty in it.
Rain never pretended to be anything else.
It fell.
It nourished.
It vanished.
Simple.
Certain.
Unlike people.
Unlike Courts.
Unlike power.
She stood at the apartment window one morning watching droplets slide down the glass.
Below, the city was waking.
Merchants arranged displays.
Children hurried toward lessons.
Bakers opened their doors.
The familiar rhythms soothed her.
Behind her, Dagda stirred awake.
The mattress creaked softly.
Moments later she felt his arms circle her waist.
His chin rested against her shoulder.
Neither spoke.
They watched the rain together.
After a time, Dagda sighed.
"The city feels different."
Morrigan's gaze remained on the street.
"I know."
The answer surprised him.
"You feel it too?"
"Yes."
The word emerged quietly.
Neither rushed to explain.
Because neither fully understood.
It was not fear.
Not yet.
Simply a subtle tension beneath daily life.
Like a string pulled too tightly.
Not broken.
But strained.
Waiting.
The sensation had been growing for months.
Small things.
Insignificant things.
Humans asking unusual questions.
Merchants discussing industrial innovations.
Travelers bringing stories from distant cities.
Nothing alarming.
Nothing obvious.
Yet the feeling persisted.
Something was changing.
Neither knew what.
________________________________________
The first rumors arrived from Steelmills.
A merchant brought them.
Human.
Middle-aged.
Exhausted from travel.
He rented a room above the tavern near the western gate.
By evening half the city had gathered to hear his stories.
Dagda listened from the edge of the crowd.
Morrigan stood beside him.
Rain tapped softly against awnings overhead.
The merchant spoke animatedly.
"They've built machines unlike anything I've seen."
The listeners exchanged curious glances.
"What sort of machines?" someone asked.
The merchant spread his arms.
"Huge things."
His eyes widened.
"Pumps. Presses. Lifts."
Excitement spread through the gathering.
"Magic?"
The merchant hesitated.
"No."
That single answer silenced everyone.
"No?"
"No."
The merchant shook his head.
"They claim it runs on steam."
Several people laughed.
Others frowned.
Steam.
The idea sounded absurd.
Useful for heating water.
Useful for cooking.
Useful for little else.
Yet the merchant remained serious.
"I saw them."
The crowd quieted again.
"They move without Fae assistance."
Someone scoffed.
"Impossible."
The merchant met the challenge immediately.
"I thought so too."
A pause.
"Until I watched one lift stone blocks heavier than ten Earth-touched Fae combined."
The laughter vanished.
Morrigan felt Dagda tense beside her.
Steam.
The word lingered.
Uncomfortable.
Wrong.
Not because machines were impossible.
Because the story felt incomplete.
She could not explain why.
Only that something beneath it felt hidden.
Obscured.
The shadows around her feet shifted restlessly.
Listening.
Suspicious.
________________________________________
That night sleep came slowly.
Rain continued tapping against the window.
Dagda lay awake staring at the ceiling.
Beside him, Morrigan remained silent.
Awake as well.
Finally she spoke.
"You're thinking too loudly."
A faint smile touched his mouth.
"You can hear thoughts now?"
"No."
She rolled toward him.
"I know you."
Moonlight filtered through the curtains.
Soft silver illuminated her features.
Dagda studied her face.
Familiar.
Comforting.
Home.
"What if it's true?"
Morrigan frowned slightly.
"The machines?"
He nodded.
"Without magic?"
A long pause followed.
Morrigan considered the possibility.
Humans had always been inventive.
Curious.
Driven.
Perhaps they had discovered something new.
Perhaps.
Yet uncertainty lingered.
"If it's true," she said carefully, "then the world changes."
Dagda stared upward.
The answer settled heavily between them.
Because they both understood.
Human dependence upon Fae had always created balance.
Not equality.
Balance.
Each possessed something the other lacked.
Each needed the other.
Remove that necessity and everything shifted.
No one knew how.
No one knew where it led.
But it would change.
Morrigan reached for his hand beneath the blankets.
Their fingers intertwined.
Neither slept for a long time.
________________________________________
The Shadow People noticed changes before most.
Not because they possessed greater wisdom.
Because they paid attention.
That was their purpose.
They listened where others ignored.
Observed where others overlooked.
Watched thresholds.
Watched transitions.
Watched beginnings.
Several Akasha-touched Fae arrived in Cogsbin carrying troubling stories.
Disappearances.
Small at first.
Easy to dismiss.
A traveler missing from one village.
A craftsman gone from another.
An Earth-touched Fae failing to return home.
Nothing conclusive.
Nothing alarming.
Yet the stories accumulated.
Enough to concern Dagda.
Enough to concern Morrigan.
One evening the faction gathered in a rented hall.
Candles flickered along wooden tables.
Rain drummed against the roof overhead.
Faces filled the room.
Concerned faces.
Uneasy faces.
Waiting faces.
Dagda stood.
The hall quieted.
"We do not know what is happening."
No one interrupted.
No one argued.
"We know only that people are disappearing."
A murmur spread.
Fear.
Frustration.
Confusion.
Dagda continued.
"We will not spread panic."
The room settled again.
"We will gather facts."
Morrigan watched from beside him.
Pride warmed her chest.
Dagda never spoke to impress.
Never manipulated emotions.
Never sought applause.
He simply told the truth.
Even when the truth was incomplete.
Especially then.
A young woman raised her hand.
"What if the Courts are involved?"
The question hung heavily.
Dagda exchanged a glance with Morrigan.
Neither answered immediately.
Because neither knew.
Eventually Morrigan spoke.
"We investigate everything."
The answer satisfied no one.
But it was honest.
And honesty mattered.
________________________________________
Summer arrived.
The city grew warmer.
Busier.
Louder.
The airships increased in number.
Trade expanded.
Merchants celebrated prosperity.
Yet beneath the excitement something darker continued growing.
The disappearances accelerated.
Not dramatically.
Steadily.
Enough to notice.
Enough to worry.
One afternoon a Jala Fae failed to return from neighboring settlements.
Three days later another vanished.
Then another.
No bodies.
No evidence.
No explanations.
Only absence.
The uncertainty felt worse than certainty.
Fear flourished where answers did not exist.
Dagda spent increasing hours investigating.
Following rumors.
Interviewing witnesses.
Searching roads and forests.
Morrigan joined him whenever possible.
Together they traveled beyond the city.
Through farmland.
Through villages.
Through forgotten roads winding between hills.
Everywhere they found the same thing.
Questions.
No answers.
Until late one evening.
________________________________________
The road home wound through dense woods.
Moonlight filtered through leaves overhead.
The forest felt unusually quiet.
No birds.
No insects.
Only wind.
Morrigan noticed first.
Her hand tightened around Dagda's arm.
"Stop."
They halted immediately.
The shadows around them stirred.
Listening.
Searching.
Dagda closed his eyes.
Extended his awareness.
The forest felt wrong.
Not dangerous.
Violated.
A subtle distinction.
But important.
They moved cautiously forward.
One step.
Then another.
The smell reached them first.
Blood.
Old blood.
Faint.
Nearly gone.
Morrigan's stomach tightened.
The shadows gathered closer.
Silent.
Watchful.
Several minutes later they found it.
A clearing.
Recently disturbed.
Grass flattened.
Earth churned.
And in the center—
A stain.
Dark.
Large.
Too large.
Dagda crouched beside it.
His expression hardened.
No body.
No remains.
Only blood.
And something worse.
Absence.
The stain felt hollow.
Empty.
As though something essential had been removed.
Extracted.
Taken.
Morrigan felt cold.
The night suddenly seemed darker.
Dagda touched the ground.
Closed his eyes.
The shadows answered.
Fragments emerged.
Echoes.
Not memories.
Impressions.
Fear.
Pain.
Restraint.
A desperate struggle.
Then—
Nothing.
A void.
Clean.
Artificial.
Wrong.
Dagda opened his eyes.
The color had drained from his face.
Morrigan knelt beside him.
"What did you see?"
His answer emerged barely above a whisper.
"They drained someone."
Silence.
The words seemed impossible.
Unreasonable.
Monstrous.
Morrigan stared at him.
"What?"
Dagda swallowed.
"The Essence."
The shadows around them recoiled.
As though even they disliked the truth.
"They took all of it."
Neither moved.
Neither spoke.
The forest remained silent around them.
Because suddenly everything made sense.
The disappearances.
The rumors.
The machines.
Steam.
The explanation felt obvious now.
Terrible.
But obvious.
If humans had discovered how to use Essence...
If they had discovered how to extract it...
If they had discovered how much power it contained...
Morrigan's chest tightened painfully.
"No."
The word escaped before she could stop it.
Dagda remained silent.
Because he could not honestly disagree.
________________________________________
They returned to Cogsbin before dawn.
The city still slept.
Rain clouds gathered overhead.
The streets stood empty.
Yet everything looked different now.
Every building.
Every lamp.
Every smiling face remembered from previous days.
Not because the city had changed.
Because knowledge had.
The apartment above the bakery felt smaller.
The walls felt closer.
The air felt heavier.
Morrigan stood by the window as gray morning light spread across rooftops.
Dagda remained seated nearby.
Neither had slept.
Neither could.
Eventually Morrigan spoke.
"If humans know..."
Her voice faltered.
Dagda finished the thought.
"They'll want more."
Silence followed.
The truth lingered between them.
Sharp.
Unavoidable.
Because greed never stopped voluntarily.
It expanded.
Consumed.
Demanded.
And Essence represented power unlike anything mortals had ever possessed.
Outside, the city continued waking.
Merchants opened doors.
Children crossed streets.
Bakers prepared bread.
Ordinary life continued.
Yet beneath it something had already broken.
A single crack.
Tiny.
Nearly invisible.
But growing.
Neither Dagda nor Morrigan realized how quickly it would spread.
Neither realized they were witnessing the final months of the world they loved.
The first crack had appeared.
The fracture would not stop there.
