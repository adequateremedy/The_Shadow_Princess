The first thing Morrigan noticed about Cogsbin was the smell.
Warm bread.
Not the scent itself, but the way it lingered.
The city seemed wrapped in it.
Fresh loaves cooling on windowsills.
Sweet rolls dusted with sugar.
Butter melting into crusts still steaming from ovens.
The fragrance drifted through narrow streets and crowded squares, mingling with sea air carried inland by the wind.
It felt alive.
Human.
Comforting.
Dagda noticed something else.
No one stared.
Not really.
Humans passed Fae in the streets without panic.
Children chased one another through market lanes while a Seelie woman sold flowers beside a human carpenter.
A Jala Fae stood laughing with fishermen near the docks.
A Vayu Fae helped guide a massive airship toward its mooring tower using currents only he could feel.
Life simply continued.
Neither race pretending the other did not exist.
Neither race fearing the other.
The sight unsettled him more than hostility ever had.
Peace always felt fragile.
Peace always felt temporary.
Yet as he watched a human baker hand a warm loaf to an elderly Fae woman without hesitation, he found himself wanting to believe.
Just once.
Morrigan seemed to sense his thoughts.
Her fingers brushed his hand.
"Stop waiting for disaster."
He glanced sideways.
"I wasn't."
"You were."
A smile tugged at her mouth.
"You get that expression."
"What expression?"
"The one that says you're trying to solve problems that haven't happened yet."
Dagda exhaled.
"I dislike being predictable."
"Then stop making the same face every time you're worried."
The answer earned a reluctant laugh.
The sound surprised both of them.
Because it had become rare.
Too many years spent navigating Court politics.
Too many years spent carrying the burdens of others.
Too many years spent surviving.
Now, standing beneath bright morning skies in a city that smelled like bread and rain, laughter felt unfamiliar.
Wonderful.
Dangerous.
Human.
________________________________________
The bakery belonged to a widower named Eamon.
Human.
Gray-haired.
Broad-shouldered.
Constantly dusted in flour.
The first time he met them, he stared at Morrigan's shadow for nearly ten seconds.
The darkness pooled strangely around her feet.
Watching.
Listening.
Existing in ways ordinary shadows did not.
Eamon finally scratched his beard.
"You planning on paying rent?"
Dagda blinked.
"Yes."
"Good."
The baker nodded.
"Then I don't particularly care what follows you around."
That was the entire conversation.
Three days later they moved into the rooms above his bakery.
The apartment was small.
One bedroom.
A kitchen barely large enough for two people.
Wooden floors that creaked with every step.
A single window overlooking the street below.
Morrigan loved it immediately.
Dagda could not understand why.
"The ceiling leaks."
"A little."
"The floors are uneven."
"They have character."
"The bedroom is tiny."
She smiled.
"We only need one."
Dagda groaned.
Morrigan laughed.
The sound filled every corner of the room.
The apartment seemed larger afterward.
________________________________________
Life settled into rhythms.
Simple rhythms.
Beautiful rhythms.
The sort of life neither had realized they wanted until they were living it.
Each morning began before sunrise.
The bakery ovens awakened first.
Heat rose through the floorboards.
The smell of baking bread drifted upstairs.
Dagda usually woke before Morrigan.
He would lie quietly beside her and listen.
Listen to her breathing.
Listen to distant carts rattling across cobblestones.
Listen to the city waking.
The moments became precious.
A private stillness before the world demanded anything.
Sometimes Morrigan remained asleep long enough for sunlight to touch her face.
Dagda always watched.
Never intentionally.
Never because he meant to.
Simply because he couldn't help it.
She looked younger while sleeping.
Softer.
Untouched by responsibility.
Untouched by expectation.
Untouched by the endless demands of leadership.
The woman he loved.
Not the symbol others saw.
Not the leader.
Just Morrigan.
Eventually her eyes would open.
Silver meeting silver.
The first thing she always did was smile.
Every morning.
Without fail.
As though she genuinely liked waking beside him.
As though she considered it a gift.
The realization never stopped affecting him.
________________________________________
Their faction continued growing.
Not rapidly.
Not dramatically.
Steadily.
Word spread.
Shadow-touched Fae arrived from distant regions seeking answers.
Seeking belonging.
Seeking understanding.
Some stayed.
Some moved on.
All were welcomed.
Meetings often occurred in rented halls after sunset.
No banners.
No grand declarations.
Just conversations.
Shared meals.
Shared concerns.
Shared experiences.
The Shadow People did not think of themselves as extraordinary.
Most had spent their lives feeling strange.
Alone.
Misunderstood.
The faction existed because they were tired of carrying that loneliness individually.
Dagda listened more than he spoke.
Morrigan did the same.
Leadership never interested either of them.
Yet people naturally gathered around them.
Asked questions.
Requested guidance.
Sought reassurance.
Not because of power.
Because of trust.
The distinction mattered.
________________________________________
One evening a young Akasha-touched Fae named Cian arrived.
Barely past maturity.
Thin.
Terrified.
His hands shook throughout the meeting.
Morrigan noticed immediately.
After everyone else left, she approached him.
"You haven't eaten."
The young Fae lowered his gaze.
"I wasn't hungry."
"You were."
Silence.
Morrigan sat beside him.
The hall had emptied.
Only candlelight remained.
The shadows along the walls stirred softly.
Patient.
Listening.
Cian swallowed.
"My family says something is wrong with me."
Morrigan waited.
"My father says I frighten people."
The words emerged quietly.
Ashamed.
Broken.
Morrigan understood that tone.
She remembered it.
Remembered hearing similar words herself.
Years ago.
Before Dagda.
Before understanding.
Before belonging.
She leaned forward.
"Do you frighten people?"
The young Fae hesitated.
"Sometimes."
"Why?"
"My shadows move."
The confession nearly shattered him.
Tears gathered.
His hands clenched.
"I don't mean for them to."
Morrigan glanced toward the darkness pooling beneath nearby tables.
The shadows shifted gently.
Responding.
Listening.
Aware.
She smiled.
"So do mine."
Cian froze.
Slowly he raised his eyes.
Hope appeared there.
Small.
Fragile.
But real.
Morrigan rested a hand against his shoulder.
"You're not broken."
The words struck him harder than any speech.
Tears finally escaped.
The shadows around him eased.
For the first time since entering the hall, they stopped trembling.
Dagda watched from across the room.
His chest tightened.
Not from sadness.
From pride.
Morrigan never realized how many people she saved.
Sometimes merely by existing.
________________________________________
Months passed.
Seasons shifted.
Life continued.
The city accepted them.
Humans greeted them by name.
Shopkeepers reserved tables for them.
Children waved when they passed.
Some evenings Dagda helped fishermen mend nets.
Other nights Morrigan spent hours speaking with human mothers about recipes and household troubles.
The interactions were ordinary.
That was precisely what made them precious.
The world felt stable.
Predictable.
Safe.
Even Queen Mab seemed distant.
Like a memory.
Like a chapter already finished.
For a while, peace became believable.
________________________________________
One autumn afternoon Morrigan stood at the apartment window watching leaves drift through the street below.
Gold.
Amber.
Crimson.
The city glowed beneath fading sunlight.
Dagda entered carrying two cups of tea.
She accepted one.
Neither spoke immediately.
The silence felt comfortable.
Outside, a group of children raced between market stalls.
One of them was human.
The other clearly Fae.
Neither cared.
Neither noticed.
Morrigan smiled.
Dagda followed her gaze.
"You like watching them."
"They don't know."
"Know what?"
She considered the question.
"The difference."
The children disappeared around a corner.
Still laughing.
Still chasing one another.
Still innocent.
Dagda nodded.
"Maybe that's good."
Morrigan sipped her tea.
The warmth spread through her chest.
"Maybe."
Yet something lingered behind the answer.
Something uneasy.
A feeling she couldn't explain.
Like distant thunder beyond a clear horizon.
Like a shadow moving where none should exist.
Brief.
Subtle.
Gone almost immediately.
She dismissed it.
The city remained peaceful.
The people remained kind.
There was no reason for concern.
No reason at all.
________________________________________
Winter returned.
Snow dusted rooftops.
The bakery became busier.
Eamon complained constantly while secretly enjoying every moment.
One particularly cold morning Morrigan descended the stairs to find the baker wrestling with a sack of flour nearly twice his size.
She raised an eyebrow.
"You know you're losing."
The baker grunted.
"The sack disagrees."
Morrigan laughed.
The shadows beneath the flour bag rose.
Gently.
Almost invisibly.
The burden lifted.
Eamon blinked.
The sack floated.
Settled neatly beside the ovens.
The baker stared.
Then stared at Morrigan.
Then stared at the sack again.
"Useful trick."
"Useful baker."
Eamon nodded.
A fair exchange.
Neither mentioned it again.
That was Cogsbin.
Magic existed.
Life continued.
No one needed explanations for everything.
________________________________________
That evening snow fell heavily.
The city quieted.
Most residents remained indoors.
The bakery closed early.
Above the shop, Dagda and Morrigan sat together beside a small fire.
A blanket draped across both of them.
The room glowed with amber light.
Outside, snowflakes drifted beyond the window.
Morrigan rested her head against his shoulder.
Dagda listened to the fire crackle.
Neither felt compelled to fill the silence.
Some moments were complete exactly as they were.
Eventually Morrigan spoke.
"Do you think this lasts?"
Dagda understood immediately.
Not the evening.
Not the season.
The peace.
The life.
Everything.
He considered his answer carefully.
Outside, wind brushed against glass.
Inside, warmth surrounded them.
He wrapped an arm around her shoulders.
"I want it to."
Morrigan closed her eyes.
The honesty hurt.
Because she wanted it too.
More than she had ever wanted power.
More than recognition.
More than victory.
She wanted mornings above the bakery.
Shared meals.
Quiet conversations.
Ordinary days.
The simple miracle of waking beside the person she loved.
Nothing more.
Nothing less.
The shadows gathered softly around the room.
Listening.
Waiting.
Watching.
Beyond the city, beyond the fields, beyond the distant horizons of Eidolon, change had already begun.
Small changes.
Invisible changes.
Human curiosity becoming human ambition.
Human ambition becoming human greed.
Whispers moving through workshops.
Experiments conducted behind locked doors.
Questions asked about Essence.
Questions that should never have been asked.
Questions that would soon alter the world.
But in that moment, above the bakery, neither Dagda nor Morrigan knew.
There was only warmth.
Only love.
Only the illusion that peace might last forever.
