For three days, neither of them told anyone.
The secret belonged only to them.
A fragile thing.
A sacred thing.
Something too precious to place in the hands of the world.
Morrigan carried it quietly.
Like a candle sheltered from wind.
She woke each morning and placed her hand against her stomach.
Nothing had changed outwardly.
Nothing visible.
No movement.
No sign.
Yet she knew.
A life existed there.
Small.
Hidden.
Growing.
The realization arrived in waves.
Sometimes joy.
Sometimes disbelief.
Sometimes fear so profound it stole her breath.
A child.
Their child.
Not a dream.
Not a possibility.
A certainty.
Dagda reacted no differently.
Though he expressed it less openly.
Morrigan noticed the changes.
He watched her more.
Hovered without realizing it.
Asked if she was tired.
Asked if she was hungry.
Asked if she was comfortable.
Asked if she needed anything.
Repeatedly.
Relentlessly.
One afternoon she finally caught him carrying a stack of books down the apartment stairs.
She raised an eyebrow.
"What are you doing?"
Dagda glanced toward the books.
Then toward her.
Then back toward the books.
The guilt was immediate.
Morrigan folded her arms.
"Dagda."
"They were heavy."
"They're books."
"They're still heavy."
She stared.
He stared back.
The silence lasted several seconds.
Finally Morrigan laughed.
The sound echoed through the apartment.
Dagda attempted dignity.
Failed completely.
"I am being responsible."
"You're carrying books."
"They could have fallen."
"They were on a shelf."
"They could still have fallen."
Morrigan laughed harder.
Dagda surrendered.
A reluctant smile appeared.
Then another.
Soon both of them were laughing.
The shadows gathered nearby.
Listening.
Watching.
Almost content.
________________________________________
The news spread through the Shadow People shortly afterward.
Not intentionally.
Nothing remained secret forever.
Especially good news.
Especially among people who cared.
The first reactions varied.
Surprise.
Joy.
Concern.
Excitement.
Fear.
The last emotion appeared most often.
Not because anyone disliked the news.
Because everyone understood what it meant.
Pregnant Fae carried potent Essence.
Everyone knew that.
Everyone.
Including humans.
The realization lingered behind every congratulations.
Every smile.
Every celebration.
A shadow beneath happiness.
A reminder.
The world outside was changing.
And Morrigan suddenly possessed something many would kill to obtain.
Neither she nor Dagda discussed it openly.
Yet both thought about it constantly.
________________________________________
Summer drifted toward autumn.
The city changed with it.
Leaves began gathering along streets.
The evenings cooled.
The air carried hints of distant rain.
And everywhere new machines appeared.
Larger.
More numerous.
More ambitious.
Factories expanded.
Warehouses multiplied.
Investors celebrated.
Newspapers praised progress.
Steam had become the language of the age.
The promise of the future.
The symbol of prosperity.
The lie continued growing.
Most humans never questioned it.
Why would they?
The machines worked.
Industry flourished.
Jobs increased.
Goods became cheaper.
Lives improved.
At least for some.
At least for now.
The cost remained hidden.
Buried beneath walls.
Locked behind gates.
Concealed from public view.
Fae continued disappearing.
And no one in power seemed interested in asking why.
________________________________________
The first attack happened at dusk.
Not upon Morrigan.
Upon another pregnant Fae.
The news arrived through frightened whispers.
A Seelie woman traveling with her family had been taken outside Steelmills.
Her husband found dead.
Her escort missing.
The woman herself gone.
Vanished.
The story spread rapidly.
By midnight every Shadow Person in Cogsbin knew.
Morrigan sat silently at the kitchen table.
The apartment felt unusually cold.
Dagda stood near the window.
Watching rain slide down the glass.
Neither spoke for a long time.
The silence hurt.
Because neither needed words.
Both understood.
The rules had changed.
Before, Fae had been hunted.
Now pregnant Fae were targets.
Prizes.
Resources.
The thought made Morrigan physically ill.
Dagda finally broke the silence.
"We need to leave."
The words settled heavily between them.
Morrigan lowered her gaze.
The apartment suddenly seemed smaller.
The walls more precious.
The familiar creaking floorboards.
The scent of bread drifting up from below.
The view from the window.
Every ordinary detail.
Every cherished routine.
Home.
She closed her eyes.
Because she already knew he was right.
________________________________________
That night neither slept.
Rain continued falling.
Soft.
Persistent.
Morrigan sat beside the window wrapped in a blanket.
Dagda occupied the chair nearby.
The room glowed with firelight.
Outside, lanterns shimmered across wet streets.
Cogsbin looked beautiful.
Peaceful.
Safe.
The illusion felt cruel.
Eventually Morrigan spoke.
"I hate this."
Dagda nodded.
"I know."
"No."
Her voice trembled slightly.
"I really hate this."
He looked up.
Morrigan stared through the glass.
Tears gathered despite her efforts.
"We built a life here."
The words emerged quietly.
Broken.
Honest.
"We finally found somewhere that felt like ours."
Dagda's chest tightened.
Because she was right.
Every word.
The bakery.
The neighbors.
The city.
The mornings.
The ordinary days.
The simple happiness.
They had fought so hard for it.
Sacrificed so much for it.
And now they were losing it.
Not because they failed.
Not because they made mistakes.
Because greed had arrived.
Because fear had arrived.
Because history had chosen them as another casualty.
Dagda rose.
Crossed the room.
Knelt beside her chair.
For a moment neither spoke.
Then he took her hand.
Held it tightly.
"We still have each other."
Morrigan laughed weakly through tears.
"That's a terribly unfair thing to say."
His brow furrowed.
"Why?"
"Because it's true."
The answer nearly broke him.
He rested his forehead against hers.
Closed his eyes.
The shadows gathered quietly around them.
Protective.
Faithful.
Home.
Whatever happened next, they would face it together.
That certainty remained.
Even as everything else crumbled.
________________________________________
The following week brought new laws.
Harsher laws.
Faster laws.
Fae movement became restricted.
Travel permits were required.
Inspections increased.
Curfews appeared.
Official explanations remained polite.
Reasonable.
Temporary.
Necessary.
The language never changed.
Only the severity.
Morrigan watched a group of guards questioning a young Fae family near the market square.
Their crime appeared to be traveling without documentation.
The children looked frightened.
The parents looked exhausted.
The guards looked bored.
Routine.
Procedure.
Nothing personal.
That frightened her more than cruelty.
Cruelty at least acknowledged suffering.
Routine ignored it.
________________________________________
Three days later Eamon came upstairs after closing.
The old baker rarely visited without reason.
That alone worried Dagda.
The moment Eamon entered, his expression confirmed it.
Something was wrong.
The baker removed his cap.
Shifted awkwardly.
Looked older than usual.
Finally he sighed.
"They came by."
Silence.
Dagda exchanged a glance with Morrigan.
"Who?"
Eamon looked uncomfortable.
"The city inspectors."
No one spoke.
The baker rubbed flour-stained hands together.
"They asked questions."
Morrigan's stomach tightened.
"What kind of questions?"
The answer came slowly.
Reluctantly.
"About you."
The room grew still.
Rain tapped softly against the windows.
Somewhere below, cooling ovens crackled faintly.
Eamon avoided their eyes.
"They wanted to know if you had visitors."
A pause.
"If you traveled."
Another pause.
"If any unusual Fae came here."
The words hung heavily in the air.
Dagda felt anger rising.
Not explosive anger.
Cold anger.
The dangerous kind.
Eamon finally looked up.
"I didn't tell them anything."
Morrigan smiled sadly.
"You shouldn't have had to choose."
The baker shrugged.
"I wasn't choosing."
He glanced between them.
"You live here."
The simplicity of the statement hurt more than any speech.
Because that was all it should have been.
Neighbors.
Friends.
Community.
Nothing complicated.
Nothing political.
Nothing controversial.
Yet the world increasingly disagreed.
________________________________________
After Eamon left, silence filled the apartment.
The rain had stopped.
The city beyond the windows seemed distant.
Unreachable.
Morrigan rested both hands upon her stomach.
An unconscious habit now.
One she barely noticed.
Dagda watched her.
The firelight reflected softly across her face.
Something had changed since the pregnancy.
Not her appearance.
Something deeper.
A new gravity.
A new tenderness.
A new vulnerability she rarely showed anyone else.
The sight filled him with equal measures of love and terror.
Because suddenly he understood what he stood to lose.
Not abstractly.
Not theoretically.
Specifically.
Morrigan.
Their child.
Their future.
The realization sharpened everything.
Every threat.
Every risk.
Every decision.
Outside, a distant whistle echoed across the city.
Factory machinery.
Steam machinery.
The sound lingered through the night.
Artificial.
Industrial.
Hungry.
Morrigan listened until it faded.
Then she looked toward Dagda.
"We don't have much time."
He nodded.
"No."
The answer emerged quietly.
Because both knew it.
The city was closing around them.
The walls were tightening.
The lie was becoming reality.
And somewhere beyond Cogsbin, beyond the factories and roads and growing industries of Eidolon, destiny had already begun moving toward them.
Toward Morrigan.
Toward the child she carried.
Toward the future the realm was prepared to survive.
The shadows knew it.
They gathered silently around the apartment.
Watching.
Waiting.
As though listening for footsteps not yet heard.
As though sensing a storm still hidden beyond the horizon.
And deep within Morrigan, beneath flesh and blood and Essence, something stirred for the very first time.
Small.
Gentle.
Almost imperceptible.
Yet unmistakably alive.
Brigid had moved.
And the world shifted with her.
