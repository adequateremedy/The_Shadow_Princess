The registration office opened beneath bright skies.
That fact angered Morrigan more than it should have.
The morning was beautiful.
Sunlight spilled across rooftops.
Birds sang from chimneys.
The scent of fresh bread drifted through the streets.
Everything appeared normal.
Yet a line of Fae already stretched beyond the government building.
Waiting.
Silent.
Watching.
The sight felt wrong.
Not because they stood together.
Because they stood uncertain.
Many held official notices in trembling hands.
Others stared at the ground.
Some glanced nervously toward nearby guards.
No one smiled.
No one spoke loudly.
Fear had a way of lowering voices.
Dagda and Morrigan stood across the street observing.
The shadows gathered quietly around their feet.
Neither had registered.
Neither intended to.
At least not yet.
Morrigan watched an elderly Earth-touched Fae approach the entrance.
His shoulders were bent with age.
A cane supported his weight.
A human clerk accepted his papers without looking at him.
The interaction lasted only seconds.
Yet something about it felt degrading.
Reduced.
As though a life spanning centuries had become an entry in a ledger.
A name.
A number.
A category.
Nothing more.
Dagda exhaled slowly.
"They already know most of these people."
Morrigan nodded.
"That's not the point."
No.
It wasn't.
The purpose was never information.
The purpose was ownership.
The city simply had not realized it yet.
________________________________________
The Shadow People refused registration.
Not officially.
No declarations were issued.
No public statements made.
The choice spread naturally.
Quietly.
One member refused.
Then another.
Then another.
By week's end most of the faction had avoided the process entirely.
The response from officials came swiftly.
Not violently.
Not openly.
Something subtler.
Meetings canceled unexpectedly.
Business permits delayed.
Employment opportunities withdrawn.
Requests denied.
No explanations offered.
The pattern became impossible to ignore.
Punishment disguised as bureaucracy.
Pressure disguised as policy.
The city was changing.
And everyone could feel it.
Even those unwilling to admit it.
________________________________________
Morrigan noticed it first among the humans she knew.
Not hatred.
Distance.
A merchant who once greeted her warmly now seemed distracted.
A neighbor who once stopped to chat suddenly found reasons to hurry elsewhere.
A baker who once offered samples now avoided eye contact.
Small things.
Tiny things.
Yet they accumulated.
The wounds inflicted by society rarely arrived all at once.
They arrived gradually.
Until one day the distance became undeniable.
One afternoon Morrigan entered a textile shop she had visited for years.
The owner, a human woman named Clara, looked up from her counter.
Recognition flickered across her face.
Followed by discomfort.
The transition lasted less than a second.
Morrigan still saw it.
Still felt it.
"Good afternoon."
Clara smiled.
Too quickly.
Too carefully.
"Good afternoon."
Silence followed.
Unnatural silence.
The kind that had never existed between them before.
Morrigan browsed fabrics.
Asked questions.
Received answers.
Everything remained polite.
Everything remained civil.
Everything remained wrong.
When she finally left, the ache followed her into the street.
Not because Clara hated her.
Because Clara was afraid.
And fear had begun doing what hatred alone never could.
Separating people who once trusted one another.
________________________________________
That evening she sat beside the apartment window.
Rain tapped softly against the glass.
The city below shimmered beneath lanternlight.
Dagda entered carrying tea.
He immediately noticed her expression.
Without speaking, he handed her a cup.
Then sat beside her.
For several minutes neither spoke.
Finally Morrigan sighed.
"They're changing."
Dagda nodded.
"I know."
The answer came quietly.
Not argumentative.
Not dismissive.
Simply sad.
Morrigan stared through the rain.
"Some of them don't even realize it."
"No."
"They still think they're good people."
Dagda considered that.
Then nodded again.
"Most of them probably are."
The answer surprised her.
She turned toward him.
Dagda stared into his tea.
Thoughtful.
Tired.
"They don't see where the road leads."
Rain continued striking the window.
Soft.
Steady.
Morrigan looked away again.
Because that was what frightened her most.
Not villains.
Not monsters.
Ordinary people.
Convincing themselves they were doing nothing wrong.
History often began there.
________________________________________
The disappearances accelerated.
No longer one or two each month.
Now several each week.
Fae vanished from roads.
From villages.
From cities.
Entire families disappeared overnight.
The stories spread faster than officials could suppress them.
Fear blossomed.
The Shadow People worked tirelessly.
Searching.
Tracking.
Guiding refugees.
Providing safe houses.
Warning settlements.
The work never ended.
Dagda spent most days traveling.
Most nights investigating.
Sleep became rare.
Rest became impossible.
One evening he returned home after nearly four days away.
Morrigan found him asleep in a chair.
Still wearing his cloak.
Boots muddy.
Head tilted awkwardly against the wall.
The sight broke her heart.
Because she knew he had not intended to sleep there.
He had simply reached his limit.
Quietly she knelt beside him.
His face looked older.
Exhaustion carved new lines beneath his eyes.
Stress lingered in every muscle.
The weight he carried had become visible.
Morrigan brushed a strand of hair from his forehead.
Dagda stirred.
Slowly opened his eyes.
For a moment confusion lingered.
Then recognition.
A faint smile appeared.
"You should be in bed."
Morrigan laughed softly.
The irony earned a reluctant smile from him.
"I was going there."
"You failed."
"I noticed."
The answer sounded so genuinely annoyed that she laughed again.
A real laugh.
The first in days.
Dagda smiled wider.
The sight warmed something inside her.
Even now.
Even here.
They still found moments.
Small moments.
Moments worth protecting.
________________________________________
Summer deepened.
Heat settled across Eidolon.
The air felt heavy.
Restless.
Tense.
The city buzzed with rumors.
Some claimed Fae were sabotaging industry.
Others claimed Fae planned rebellion.
Newspapers printed increasingly hostile stories.
Not outright lies.
Half-truths.
Distortions.
Selective truths.
The most dangerous kind.
Dagda read one article while sitting in the bakery below.
His expression darkened with every line.
Eamon watched from behind the counter.
"What nonsense is it today?"
Dagda handed him the paper.
The baker squinted.
Read silently.
Then snorted.
"They make you sound terrifying."
"They're trying."
Eamon folded the paper.
Tossed it onto a nearby table.
"You're terrible at being terrifying."
Despite himself, Dagda laughed.
The baker shrugged.
"What?"
"I'm serious."
Eamon wiped flour from his hands.
"I've known you for years."
A pause.
"You apologize when people bump into you."
Dagda frowned.
"That happened once."
"Three times."
The baker pointed.
"I counted."
A reluctant smile appeared.
Brief.
But genuine.
Eamon nodded approvingly.
"There he is."
The moment passed quickly.
Yet Dagda carried it upstairs later.
A reminder.
Not everyone had changed.
Not everyone had surrendered to fear.
Some still remembered who their neighbors were.
Some still remembered kindness.
That mattered.
More than people realized.
________________________________________
Three weeks later Morrigan collapsed.
The incident happened without warning.
One moment she stood speaking with members of the faction.
The next the room tilted.
Darkness rushed forward.
Then nothing.
When consciousness returned, she found herself lying in bed.
Dagda sat nearby.
His expression revealed everything.
Fear.
Relief.
Concern.
Exhaustion.
All tangled together.
The moment their eyes met, she understood.
"You've been staring at me."
Dagda blinked.
"That's your first question?"
Morrigan smiled faintly.
"You look guilty."
His shoulders relaxed slightly.
The tension easing.
Only a little.
"You frightened me."
The admission emerged quietly.
Honest.
Raw.
Morrigan reached for his hand.
Immediately he took it.
Held it tightly.
As though afraid she might vanish.
"What happened?"
Dagda hesitated.
A strange expression crossed his face.
One she couldn't immediately identify.
Something softer.
Something uncertain.
Something almost disbelieving.
Morrigan frowned.
"What?"
The silence stretched.
Then—
"You fainted."
"I noticed."
His lips twitched.
Despite everything.
Despite the fear.
Despite the uncertainty.
The familiar humor between them remained.
Barely.
But present.
Dagda inhaled slowly.
Then exhaled.
Carefully.
Deliberately.
As though preparing himself.
"Morrigan."
The way he said her name made her chest tighten.
Something important.
Something life-changing.
Something enormous.
She sat up slightly.
Dagda's eyes never left hers.
The silence deepened.
Outside, rain tapped softly against the roof.
The bakery ovens crackled below.
The world seemed to hold its breath.
Finally he spoke.
"The healer examined you."
Morrigan waited.
Dagda swallowed.
A smile appeared.
Small.
Disbelieving.
Beautiful.
Then came the words.
"You're pregnant."
Everything stopped.
The room.
The rain.
The world.
For a moment Morrigan simply stared at him.
Certain she had misunderstood.
Certain she had imagined it.
Pregnant.
The word echoed through her thoughts.
Impossible.
Wonderful.
Terrifying.
Pregnant.
Dagda's smile widened slightly.
Not because he found the moment amusing.
Because he could not contain the emotion.
The disbelief.
The joy.
The fear.
All of it.
Morrigan felt tears gathering unexpectedly.
Not sadness.
Something larger.
Something beyond language.
Her hands trembled.
Slowly she placed one against her stomach.
Nothing felt different.
Nothing felt changed.
Yet everything had changed.
Everything.
Dagda moved closer.
Carefully.
Almost reverently.
As though afraid reality might shatter if touched too quickly.
Morrigan looked at him.
Really looked.
And saw tears in his eyes as well.
That finally broke her.
She laughed.
Then cried.
Then laughed again.
Dagda joined her.
The sound filled the apartment.
Filled the silence.
Filled the future waiting just beyond the horizon.
Outside, the city continued changing.
The laws continued tightening.
The disappearances continued growing.
The lie called steam continued spreading.
But for one precious moment none of it mattered.
There was only them.
Only wonder.
Only the tiny life beginning quietly within her.
And neither realized that life would alter the fate of realms.
